**Algebra** is the branch of mathematics that studies patterns, relationships, and structures using symbols. Unlike arithmetic, which works with specific numbers, algebra introduces variables—usually letters such as _x_, _y_, or _n_—to represent unknown quantities or values that can change.

At its core, algebra provides a universal language for mathematics. It allows us to describe general rules, solve problems involving unknowns, and model real-world phenomena ranging from population growth and finance to computer algorithms and physics.

Some of the fundamental questions algebra seeks to answer are:

- How can unknown quantities be determined?
    
- How can mathematical relationships be expressed in a general form?
    
- What structures govern numbers, equations, and transformations?
    

When you solve for an unknown value, graph a function, calculate compound interest, or write a computer program, you are using algebraic thinking.

Algebra is broadly divided into two major domains: **Elementary Algebra** and **Higher Algebra**.

#### Elementary Algebra

This is the foundation of algebra and is typically the first encounter students have with symbolic mathematics.

- **Expressions and Variables:** Mathematical statements involving variables and operations, such as $3x + 5$ .
    
- **Equations and Inequalities:** Relationships involving unknown quantities that can be solved, such as $2x + 3 = 11$ .
    
- **Functions:** Rules that assign each input exactly one output, often written as $f(x)$ .
    
- **Polynomials:** Expressions involving variables raised to whole-number powers, such as $x^2 + 3x - 4$ .
    
- **Factoring and Simplification:** Techniques for rewriting expressions into simpler or more useful forms.
    

#### Higher Algebra

As algebra develops, the focus shifts from solving equations to studying the deeper structures underlying mathematics.

- **Linear Algebra:** The study of vectors, matrices, linear transformations, and systems of linear equations. It forms the mathematical foundation of computer graphics, machine learning, quantum mechanics, and engineering.
    
- **Abstract Algebra:** The study of algebraic structures such as groups, rings, and fields. Rather than studying specific numbers, abstract algebra investigates the rules and operations that mathematical systems obey.
    
- **Boolean Algebra:** A system of algebra based on true and false values, fundamental to digital circuits and computer science.
    
- **Algebraic Geometry:** The study of geometric objects defined by polynomial equations.
    
- **Commutative Algebra:** The study of rings and ideals, providing the algebraic foundation for modern geometry and number theory.
    

#### Equations: The Central Object of Algebra

Historically, algebra emerged from the desire to solve equations. Early algebra focused on finding solutions to linear and quadratic equations, but modern algebra studies equations of all kinds.

For example:

$$ax^2+bx+c=0$$

The search for general methods to solve polynomial equations led to some of the most profound discoveries in mathematics and ultimately gave rise to abstract algebra.

#### The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra

One of the central results in mathematics is the **Fundamental Theorem of Algebra**, which states that every non-constant polynomial with complex coefficients has at least one complex root.

This theorem implies that polynomial equations can always be completely factored over the complex numbers, making the complex number system algebraically complete.

#### Algebraic Structures

Modern algebra is built upon the study of abstract structures that capture common patterns across many mathematical systems.

- **Groups:** Structures that describe symmetry and transformations.
    
- **Rings:** Systems where addition and multiplication can be performed.
    
- **Fields:** Number systems where addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division are all possible (except division by zero).
    
- **Vector Spaces:** Collections of vectors that can be scaled and added according to specific rules.
    

These structures provide a unifying framework connecting geometry, number theory, topology, cryptography, and physics.

#### The Role of Algebra in Modern Mathematics

If calculus is often described as the mathematics of change, algebra is the mathematics of structure. Nearly every area of modern mathematics relies on algebraic ideas to describe relationships and uncover hidden patterns.

The ultimate goal of modern algebra is to understand the abstract structures that govern mathematical systems, revealing deep connections between seemingly unrelated areas of mathematics. Through concepts such as symmetry, transformations, and algebraic structures, algebra serves as one of the central pillars upon which modern mathematics is built.
