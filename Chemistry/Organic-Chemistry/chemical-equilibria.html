Chemical Equilibria

In chemistry, irreversible reactions like fires and explosions often get all the attention. However, many, if not most, reactions don't go to completion. Instead, the products can react to regenerate the starting materials, establishing a reversible reaction. These equilibria are constantly at work all around us, often invisibly. At any given moment, a vast number of protons in a can of Diet Coke are exchanging with water molecules, but because there is no net change, the process goes unnoticed.

Chemical equilibrium is a central topic in organic chemistry. Many reactions you will study are equilibrium processes. This concept also extends to biological systems, such as how substrates bind to enzymes or how molecules reversibly change their shape.

The Basics of Chemical Equilibrium

So, what does it mean for a system to be in chemical equilibrium? Here are the fundamental principles:

  • An equilibrium can only be established if a significant reverse reaction is possible.
  • Once equilibrium is reached, the concentrations of reactants and products remain constant over time.
  • At equilibrium, the change in Gibbs free energy (ΔG) is zero, meaning there is no net driving force for the reaction in either direction.

You were likely introduced to equilibria through acid-base reactions. The reaction of a strong acid like HCl with water is essentially irreversible. In contrast, a weak acid like acetic acid (CH₃COOH) reacts with water to form H₃O⁺ and CH₃COO⁻, but this reaction is reversible.

The extent of this reaction is described by the equilibrium constant (Kₐ). A larger Kₐ value indicates that the reaction proceeds further to the right, resulting in a higher concentration of H₃O⁺ and a stronger acid. To make these numbers easier to work with, chemists often use pKₐ, which is the negative logarithm of Kₐ. Due to the negative sign, stronger acids have lower pKₐ values, while weaker acids have higher pKₐ values.

Equilibrium in Everyday Life

Equilibrium reactions are happening around you constantly. For example, inside a can of soda, an equilibrium exists between dissolved (and pressurized) carbon dioxide and water, which forms carbonic acid (H₂CO₃). At the same time, carbonic acid dissociates back into carbon dioxide and water.

The Link Between Equilibrium and Energy (ΔG)

At equilibrium, there is no net driving force, and the change in Gibbs free energy (ΔG) is zero. There is a powerful relationship between the equilibrium constant (K) and ΔG, described by the Gibbs free energy isotherm equation:

$$ \Delta G = -RT \ln K $$

This equation allows us to calculate the energy difference between reactants and products if we know their equilibrium concentrations (K), or vice versa.

Consider a reaction A ⇌ B where at equilibrium, the ratio of products to reactants is 9:1. The equilibrium constant K is 9. Using the Gibbs equation, we find that the energy difference (ΔG) driving this reaction is only about 5.4 kJ/mol. When compared to the strength of a typical C-H bond (around 430 kJ/mol), this is a very small amount of energy. It highlights a key point: it doesn't take a large energy difference to significantly favor the formation of products. For a reaction to yield a 99:1 product-to-reactant ratio, the energy difference required is still only about 11.4 kJ/mol.

Manipulating Equilibria: Le Châtelier's Principle

In organic chemistry, the goal is often to transform one compound into another with a high yield. To achieve this in a reversible reaction, we often need to "shut down" the reverse reaction. This is described as driving a reaction forward.

This can be achieved by removing one of the products as it is formed. This manipulation disrupts the equilibrium and, according to Le Châtelier's principle, the system will shift to counteract the change—in this case, by producing more products.

For example, the formation of an imine from an aldehyde and an amine produces water as a byproduct. This reaction is reversible. If we remove the water as it forms (for instance, by adding a dehydrating agent or desiccant like MgSO₄), we prevent the reverse reaction from occurring. This forces the equilibrium to shift continuously to the right, resulting in a high yield of the imine product.

The bottom line: By understanding the products of a reversible reaction, you can choose conditions to remove one or more of them. This prevents the system from reaching equilibrium and drives the reaction toward the desired products, maximizing your yield.
