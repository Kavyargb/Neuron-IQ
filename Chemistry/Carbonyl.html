Introduction to Carbonyl Compounds

Carbonyl compounds are organic compounds containing a carbon-oxygen double bond (\(>C=O\)), known as the carbonyl group. This functional group is one of the most important in organic chemistry.

  • In aldehydes, the carbonyl carbon is bonded to at least one hydrogen atom.
  • In ketones, the carbonyl carbon is bonded to two carbon atoms.
  • In carboxylic acids, the carbonyl group is attached to a hydroxyl (\(\ce{-OH}\)) group.

Structure of the Carbonyl Group

The carbonyl carbon atom is \(sp^2\)-hybridised and forms three sigma (\(\sigma\)) bonds. The remaining p-orbital on the carbon overlaps with a p-orbital on the oxygen to form a pi (\(\pi\)) bond. The carbon and the three atoms attached to it lie in a single plane, with bond angles of approximately 120°. The \(\pi\) electron cloud is located above and below this plane.

The C=O bond is polar due to the higher electronegativity of oxygen. This makes the carbonyl carbon an electrophilic (Lewis acid) center and the carbonyl oxygen a nucleophilic (Lewis base) center. This polarity results in significant dipole moments for carbonyl compounds.

Preparation of Aldehydes and Ketones

1. By Oxidation of Alcohols

Primary alcohols are oxidized to aldehydes, and secondary alcohols are oxidized to ketones. This is a widely used method (details in Unit 7, Class XII).

2. By Dehydrogenation of Alcohols

This industrial method involves passing the vapors of a primary or secondary alcohol over a heavy metal catalyst (Ag or Cu) at high temperatures. Primary alcohols yield aldehydes, and secondary alcohols yield ketones.

3. From Hydrocarbons: Ozonolysis of Alkenes

Ozonolysis of alkenes, followed by reductive workup with zinc dust and water (\(\ce{Zn/H2O}\)), cleaves the double bond to give aldehydes, ketones, or a mixture of both, depending on the structure of the alkene.

Physical Properties

  • State: Methanal is a gas, ethanal is a volatile liquid. Other aldehydes and ketones are liquids or solids.
  • Boiling Points: Aldehydes and ketones have higher boiling points than nonpolar hydrocarbons of similar mass due to dipole-dipole interactions. However, they have lower boiling points than corresponding alcohols because they cannot form intermolecular hydrogen bonds with themselves.
  • Solubility: Lower aldehydes and ketones (e.g., methanal, ethanal, propanone) are miscible with water as they can form hydrogen bonds with water molecules. Solubility decreases as the length of the alkyl chain increases.

Chemical Reactions of Aldehydes and Ketones

1. Nucleophilic Addition Reactions

This is the characteristic reaction of the carbonyl group. A nucleophile attacks the electrophilic carbonyl carbon.

Reactivity Order: Aldehydes are generally more reactive than ketones towards nucleophilic addition. This is due to:

  1. Steric Factors: Ketones have two bulky alkyl groups hindering the approach of the nucleophile, while aldehydes have only one.
  2. Electronic Factors: The two alkyl groups in a ketone are electron-donating, reducing the positive charge (electrophilicity) on the carbonyl carbon more effectively than the single alkyl group in an aldehyde.
Examples of Nucleophilic Addition:
  • Addition of HCN: Forms cyanohydrins. The reaction is base-catalyzed.
  • Addition of \(\ce{NaHSO3}\): Forms crystalline bisulphite addition products. This is a useful method for separating and purifying aldehydes.
  • Addition of Alcohols: Aldehydes react with one equivalent of alcohol to form a hemiacetal, and with a second equivalent to form a stable acetal. Ketones react with diols (like ethylene glycol) to form cyclic ketals.
  • Addition of Ammonia and its Derivatives (\(\ce{H2N-Z}\)): This is a nucleophilic addition-elimination reaction. The initial addition product dehydrates to form a product with a C=N double bond.

2. Reduction

  • Reduction to Alcohols: Aldehydes and ketones are reduced to primary and secondary alcohols, respectively, using reducing agents like sodium borohydride (\(\ce{NaBH4}\)) or lithium aluminium hydride (\(\ce{LiAlH4}\)).
  • Reduction to Hydrocarbons:
    • Clemmensen Reduction: The carbonyl group is reduced to a methylene group (\(\ce{-CH2-}\)) using zinc-amalgam (\(\ce{Zn-Hg}\)) and concentrated HCl.
    • Wolff-Kishner Reduction: The carbonyl group is first treated with hydrazine to form a hydrazone, which is then heated with a strong base (KOH or NaOH) in a high-boiling solvent like ethylene glycol to yield a methylene group.
    \(\ce{Zn-Hg, conc. HCl}\)
    ⟶

3. Oxidation

Aldehydes are easily oxidized to carboxylic acids by both strong and mild oxidizing agents. Ketones are generally resistant to oxidation but can be oxidized under vigorous conditions.

Tests to Distinguish Aldehydes from Ketones:
  • Tollens' Test (Silver Mirror Test): Aldehydes, on warming with Tollens' reagent (ammoniacal silver nitrate), are oxidized to carboxylate ions, and the \(\ce{Ag+}\) ions are reduced to metallic silver, which forms a bright mirror on the inside of the test tube. Ketones do not give this test.
    \(\ce{R-CHO + 2[Ag(NH3)2]+ + 3OH- -> R-COO- + 2Ag v + 4NH3 + 2H2O}\)
  • Fehling's Test: Aliphatic aldehydes, on heating with Fehling's solution (a mixture of copper sulphate and sodium potassium tartrate), are oxidized to carboxylate ions, and the blue \(\ce{Cu^{2+}}\) ions are reduced to a red-brown precipitate of cuprous oxide (\(\ce{Cu2O}\)). Aromatic aldehydes and ketones do not give this test.
    \(\ce{R-CHO + 2Cu^{2+} + 5OH- -> R-COO- + Cu2O v + 3H2O}\)

4. Haloform Reaction

Aldehydes and ketones having at least one methyl group attached to the carbonyl carbon (methyl ketones) are oxidized by sodium hypohalite to give a haloform (\(\ce{CHX3}\)) and the sodium salt of a carboxylic acid with one less carbon atom. The reaction with iodine (iodoform test) gives a yellow precipitate of iodoform (\(\ce{CHI3}\)) and is used to detect the \(\ce{CH3CO-}\) group.

5. Reactions due to \(\alpha\)-Hydrogen

The \(\alpha\)-hydrogens (hydrogens on the carbon adjacent to the carbonyl group) are acidic due to the electron-withdrawing effect of the carbonyl group and the resonance stabilization of the resulting enolate anion.

  • Aldol Condensation: Aldehydes and ketones with at least one \(\alpha\)-hydrogen react in the presence of a dilute base (like NaOH) to form a \(\beta\)-hydroxy aldehyde (aldol) or a \(\beta\)-hydroxy ketone (ketol). Carboxylic Acids

Carboxylic acids are organic compounds containing the carboxyl functional group, \(\ce{-COOH}\), which is a combination of a carbonyl group and a hydroxyl group.

Preparation of Carboxylic Acids

  1. From Primary Alcohols and Aldehydes: Oxidation using common agents like \(\ce{KMnO4}\), \(\ce{K2Cr2O7}\), or CrO₃.
  2. From Alkylbenzenes: Vigorous oxidation of the alkyl side chain of an alkylbenzene with agents like acidic or alkaline \(\ce{KMnO4}\) produces benzoic acid, regardless of the length of the side chain.
  3. From Nitriles and Amides: Acidic or basic hydrolysis of nitriles or amides yields carboxylic acids.
  4. From Grignard Reagents: Grignard reagents react with carbon dioxide (dry ice) to form the salt of a carboxylic acid, which upon acidification gives the carboxylic acid. This method increases the carbon chain by one atom.

Acidity of Carboxylic Acids

Carboxylic acids are acidic because they can donate a proton to form a resonance-stabilized carboxylate anion. The negative charge in the carboxylate ion is delocalized over two electronegative oxygen atoms.

The acidity is influenced by substituents:

  • Electron-withdrawing groups (EWGs) like -Cl, -NO₂, -CN increase acidity by stabilizing the carboxylate anion.
  • Electron-donating groups (EDGs) like -CH₃, -OCH₃ decrease acidity by destabilizing the carboxylate anion.

Acidity Order: \(\ce{CF3COOH > CCl3COOH > CHCl2COOH > NO2CH2COOH > FCH2COOH > ClCH2COOH > C6H5COOH > CH3COOH}\)

Reactions of Carboxylic Acids

1. Reactions involving Cleavage of C-OH Bond

  • Formation of Anhydrides: Dehydration of carboxylic acids on heating with a dehydrating agent like \(\ce{P2O5}\).
  • Esterification: Reaction with an alcohol in the presence of an acid catalyst (\(\ce{H2SO4}\) or HCl gas) to form an ester. This is a reversible reaction known as the Fischer esterification.
  • Reaction with \(\ce{PCl5, PCl3, SOCl2}\): The -OH group is replaced by -Cl to form acyl chlorides. \(\ce{SOCl2}\) (thionyl chloride) is often preferred because the byproducts are gases.

2. Reactions involving the -COOH Group

  • Reduction: Carboxylic acids are reduced to primary alcohols by strong reducing agents like \(\ce{LiAlH4}\) or diborane (\(\ce{B2H6}\)). \(\ce{NaBH4}\) does not reduce carboxylic acids.
  • Decarboxylation: Carboxylic acids lose \(\ce{CO2}\) to form hydrocarbons when their sodium salts are heated with sodalime (NaOH + CaO).

3. Substitution in the Hydrocarbon Part (Hell-Volhard-Zelinsky Reaction)

Carboxylic acids having an \(\alpha\)-hydrogen are halogenated at the \(\alpha\)-position on treatment with chlorine or bromine in the presence of a small amount of red phosphorus to give \(\alpha\)-halocarboxylic acids.
